THEME

In recent years, the communication between China and the rest of the world has achieved an unprecedented horizon, both in width and depth. During this course, China has been exploring opportunities from all over the world; at the same time, China has also been reciprocating the world with its own values and opportunities. However, inevitably, it has been full of doubts and critiques thus far. In light of such challenges, it is now vitally important to have a heart-to-heart dialogue between China and the world — How should China and the world listen to each other to achieve “win-win”? To answer this question, we hereby proudly announce the theme of 2014 Columbia China Prospects Conference (CCPC) — China’s Dialogue with the World. Refining key elements from the previous four conferences, 2014 CCPC aims to further shed light on the opportunities and challenges China may confront when it balances between its own national transformation and the international standards.

近年来，中国与世界各国交流的广度和深度有了大幅度提升。“让中国梦携手世界梦”已经不仅仅是一个愿景，而是一个正在被践行的诺言。交流之中，中国发现了来自于全世界的机遇，也为全世界创造了源自于中国的价值。然而，在此过程之中，中国也不可避免地遭遇了质疑、碰撞与挑战。此时此刻，开展一场中国与全球的深度对话，显得尤为重要 —— 如何让世界理解中国寻求共赢的声音？中国特色是否是向多元的世界体系提出了另一种可能性？有鉴于此，本届“中国展望论坛”以“聆听中国·对话全球”为主题，将着眼于发掘转型中的中国在适应并融入当今国际体系的新模式过程中所蕴含的机遇与挑战。

Entrepreneurship in China: Integrating Technology and Market

In the current century, rapid advances in technologies have resulted in a boom in entrepreneurship. Recently, a growing number of start-ups received venture and angel investments and became flagships in their respective industries. Foreign-educated Chinese have played a major role in contributing to the success of these companies, which, in turn, fueled a growing trend among overseas Chinese to return to China and start businesses. While IT, new energy, and biotechnology are particularly favored among start-ups, problems may arise in the process of bringing these advanced technologies from Western countries to the Chinese market. How do entrepreneurs adapt new technologies to meet the specific demands of Chinese customers? How do established Chinese firms introduce foreign technologies in order to leapfrog competitors? How do Chinese private investors evaluate novel technologies and make investment decisions?

We invited investors and entrepreneurs from diverse industries and geographies to join this panel to share their perspectives on the aforementioned questions.

The Future Trajectory of China’s Higher Education: Liberal Arts Education vs. Specialized Education

In her book Not For Profit: Why Democracy Needs the Humanities (2010), American Philosopher Martha Nussbaum argues that the difficult choice between specialized education and a liberal arts education is a call to arms for all of the important stakeholders of a global education community. Indeed, the theme of this year’s CCPC Education Panel is pertinent not only to China, but also to the world at large. It will provide speakers with an exceptional platform for voicing their opinions, shedding light on current and future trends, and inspiring potential cross-border collaboration in the field of higher education. Needless to say, the event will be an intellectual feast for the audience members, whether CU or non-CU students, engineering undergraduates, or J.D. candidates.

China, the most populous country in the world, has been growing at an unprecedented rate for the past several decades. Education is undoubtedly one of the greatest driving forces of the Chinese economy. Several experts have credited specialized education focusing on particular subjects and training to prepare students for the workplace for its instrumental role in China's rise. Meanwhile, others argue that if China continues with such an educational system, it will produce generations of mercenary, docile, and technically trained "machines", rather than versatile citizens capable of introspection, critical thinking, and more importantly, innovation. They claim that a liberal arts education would help nurture creativity, civic participation, and democracy among its citizens. This two-hour panel of inspiring intellectuals and prominent leaders aims to discuss questions beyond simple economic growth considerations, and to conceptualize the key elements of higher education—whether specialized or liberal arts— that would sustain the “Great China Dream.”

The Chinese Economy: Investment Opportunities in the New Era

The year of 2014 has revealed the improving health of the advanced economies, with surging consumption and decreasing unemployment. In contrast, emerging markets have demonstrated tepid growth, with the Chinese economy at a major crossroads. The slowing growth in GDP has prompted Chinese firms to compete internationally and to revamp their business portfolios. The ongoing shifts in economic power have altered the global flow of capital, and international investors have become increasingly wary of investing in China. Despite unprecedented scrutiny of the Chinese market, regulations, and economic policies, the following question continues to cloud the thinking of global investors: Will China be the same profit engine that it once was?

The panel today consists of economists and industry leaders, who will discuss the future of the Chinese macro-economy and investment hotspots. We will examine potential effects of pending reforms on new economic models, innovations, and growth opportunities.

Chinese Enterprises: Its Path to Globalization

After 30 years of rapid economic growth since the advent of its “Reform and Opening Up Policy,” China is entering a new phase of economic transition. In a downturn, firms face increasing challenges, which will weed out weak companies in a given industry, and even entire industries that are uncompetitive. These challenges are further exacerbated by the diminishing “WTO dividends” enjoyed by China since joining the organization 10 years ago, since China first became a truly global factory. To tackle these problems and seek out new growth, Chinese firms increasingly rely upon a “Go Out Strategy”, ushering in a new era of “Chinese creation” in contrast with the “Chinese manufacturing” era of the past. Above all, the depreciation of global asset prices and the relative stability of the Chinese domestic economy since the global financial crisis have presented fascinating opportunities for cross-border acquisitions. With this global backdrop, enterprises in both the state-owned and private sectors are increasingly eager to adopt the “Go Out Strategy” and have completed numerous successful transactions. With the financial and managerial resources in China and the need for global markets and technologies, this strategy is certain to continue to blossom.

However, with the world economy still at a crossroads and trade protectionism rearing its ugly head, how will the “Go Out Strategy” fit into the new global environment? What difficulties do Chinese firms entering the global market face? How do firms strike a balance between global and domestic markets? What are the main drivers for cross-border M&As in the next decade? The panelists will share their expertise and opinions on issues related to the global expansion of Chinese firms.

The Breakthroughs of Modern Chinese Literature

Since the 1980s, Chinese literature has enjoyed a relatively free environment, with a broadening scope in terms of contents, an increase in production, and an expanding community of writers. In the 21st century in particular, numerous innovations can be observed in the creation and commercial distribution of literature. At the same time, the opening up of the Chinese mainland has offered the world greater exposure to Chinese literature, exemplified by the various international awards won by Chinese writers and the translation of Chinese works into foreign languages. Also exciting is the explosive growth in “Internet literature,” which has revolutionized the creation, distribution, and readership of literary works. In this new era, how should contemporary Chinese literature go about blazing new trails and seizing new opportunities?

What are some of the difficulties in the growth and development of contemporary Chinese literature since the 1980s? What stumbling blocks has the commercialization of Chinese works at home and abroad faced since the 2000s? What roles does Chinese literature play on the global stage? Is “Internet literature” a driving force or a hindrance to the continued development of literature as a whole? The panelists will share their outlook on and analysis of the various challenges, opportunities, and future paths that lie ahead for Chinese literature.

The Chinese Media: Its Role in the World

Both the new and the traditional media have provided unencumbered access to global information and diverse opinions, but this freedom and plurality are also accompanied by confusion and chaos. Although the Chinese media industry has traditionally served the domestic market, this will soon change. In the face of an increasing global audience, how do Chinese media companies adapt to the global landscape and co-exist with the entrenched tycoons? We will discuss the opportunities and challenges the Chinese media industry faces from the perspectives of content producers, corporate managers, and media investors as Chinese media companies enter the global stage.
Question 1: First-hand access to sources of global news
In major global events, such as the Malaysian Airlines tragedy, how can Chinese media companies obtain news at the earliest possible moment and avoid missing out on important events?
Question 2: Proliferation of cultural influence and “soft power”
The producers of globally consumed entertainment are largely restricted to the U.S., Britain, Japan, and South Korea. These TV shows and films have enabled the culture of the countries in which they are produced to penetrate into countries in which they are watched. How are Chinese media companies responding to this influence and attempting to expand their own global voice and influence?
Question 3: Leadership and guidance in terrorist incidents
With regard to such terrorist incidents as “9/11” and the incident at the “Train Station of Kunming”, how can the media best go about appropriately reporting the facts and guiding the public to understand these events objectively without manipulating or inflaming public sentiment?
Question 4: Facts or Sentiments?
With regard to unexpected events, such as the earthquake in Wenchuan and the Fukushima tsunami, should the media focus on reporting the hard facts related to the disasters or focus instead on raising awareness by reporting personal stories during these events?
Question 5: Global expectations for the Chinese media
While the Western media has long been established, the Chinese media is still in its infancy. Armed with a wealth of experience from the Western media, China is well equipped to develop novel and distinct contents and systems of distribution. What is the current image of the Chinese media across the globe? What does the established Western media expect from the globalizing Chinese media?
